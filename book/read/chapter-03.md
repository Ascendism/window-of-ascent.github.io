# Chapter 3 — Tools Are Neutral, Incentives Are Not

The argument goes like this: a hammer doesn't care whether it drives a nail into lumber or a board across a fence. The hammer is neutral. The tool itself can't be blamed for what it does. The only question is who swings it.

The argument is right up to the point where it stops asking questions.

A hammer doesn't choose. That much is true. But who decides which hammers get made, how much they cost, and where they show up on the shelf — those are decisions made by people with specific incentives. The hammer's neutrality ends at the factory gate. After that, everything is a choice.

If your target is autonomy, you need to see which choices are yours and which were made for you before you walked into the store. Which seed varieties still exist because someone kept the stock, not because the market left them on the shelf. Which tools remain available at a price that doesn't require you to sell the thing you're trying to build. Which suppliers are actually separate entities versus different brands owned by the same holding company that sets the terms across the board.

The neutrality claim seduces you into thinking that market availability equals freedom of choice. If five brands of the same underlying tool sit side by side, and you can pick any of them, you are free. The argument is tidy enough that people carry it into every debate. It also misses the question of who reduced the fifty options to five, and why the remaining five all trace back to three distribution channels that answer to the same quarterly targets.

Think about what the argument actually protects. When the conversation shifts from "is this tool good for me" to "all tools are neutral, the problem is who owns them," you have handed the tool a clean bill of health while postponing the ownership question to a future conversation that rarely happens. The neutrality framing is itself a design choice. It serves the incentive of keeping you using the tool while you debate the conditions.

You can see this pattern anywhere tools are commoditized. Consider the seed supplier who arrived at your farm gate with better packaging, a higher price, and a narrower genetic base. That supplier sold on ease of use and yield per acre — metrics that mattered when food moved through channels you could see. The farmer who bought the seed was making a rational choice given the information in front of them. The choice still served the incentive of the people who had already consolidated the breeding stock, because the metrics that got you to buy were the same metrics that let them consolidate further. The choice was yours. The range of available choices was not.

That is not an argument against every seed company. It is an argument for understanding what drives the options you're selecting from. A tool is neutral in the same way a river is neutral: the water doesn't choose its path, but the channel does. Ditches, levees, grade controls, and diversions determine where the water goes. The neutrality of the water is real. The neutrality of the system is not.

The people who benefit most from the neutrality argument are the ones who control distribution. You can see why. If tools are neutral, the only question is who operates them. That keeps the debate focused on the operator's intent rather than on whether the tool's design serves a particular outcome. A tool designed to maximize one user's engagement while extracting another user's data is still "neutral" in the strict sense — the code doesn't have intentions. But the people who wrote the pricing structure, the default settings, and the upgrade path absolutely do.

Here is where the argument gets uncomfortable for most people, because it means you have to stop trusting the range of options as evidence of freedom. You walk into a supply store and see six brands of the same underlying product at different price points. The existence of six brands feels like competition. It is only competition to the degree that the six brands answer to six different profit structures. If three of those brands answer to the same parent company, and two more source from the same supplier, and the remaining two are loss leaders designed to draw you into a subscription you'll renew next quarter, then you are looking at the illusion of choice dressed up as a well-stocked shelf.

The lesson is not that every tool is suspect. The lesson is that the word "neutral" is doing work for someone, and you need to name who that is before you decide whether the tool serves the thing you're building.

When you look at a tool through the incentive lens instead of the neutrality lens, the questions change. Instead of "Is this tool good or bad?" you ask "Who benefits when this tool succeeds at its primary function?" The primary function may not be the one advertised to you. In a two-sided market — the structure behind most of the tools people call free — one side's primary function is being the product the other side pays for. The tool is neutral about that, in the same way a scale is neutral about the fact that it's being used to price you out of something you thought you could afford.

You owe yourself a second look at the options you're selecting from. Before you choose a tool because it's available, ask what incentive shaped its availability. Before you choose it because it's cheap, ask what metric on someone else's dashboard is what you're optimizing. Cheap is not the same as free, and available is not the same as designed for you.

The neutrality claim has one honest core: tools cannot be morally culpable. A hammer is not responsible for the wall it helps build or the fence it helps raise. That much is common ground. But common ground is useful for agreement, not as the end of the argument. The next question is always about the incentive. Who benefits. Who decides. At what cost to whom.

The concentration of those decisions is the pattern worth examining next.

---

This does not require a conspiracy or the villainy of any individual actor. It is the result of market dynamics that reward scale, and scale consolidates leverage in the same way that a river concentrates its force in fewer channels as it narrows. When enough options get reduced to a handful, the people who made that reduction get to set the terms for everyone still selecting from whatever was left.

Consolidation is measurable without being editorial. Take equity markets as a starting point only because the numbers are publicly available and not disputed by index providers. The top ten holdings of the S&P 500 have accounted for an increasingly large share of the total index weight over the past decade. In mid-2024, the ten largest companies accounted for roughly forty percent of the float-weighted index — a figure that was widely discussed in financial coverage but is worth reading in the right frame. This is not a judgment about the quality of those companies. It is a structural fact about where the weight of a broad market indicator sits today versus where it sat twenty years ago.

The practical meaning for a reader choosing tools is that the market concentration this produces is not accidental. When the largest publicly traded companies in the United States represent a disproportionate slice of the entire index, those companies set pricing, availability, and default configurations that every other participant in the market must adapt to. A smaller seed company is not competing with twelve other seed companies on the basis of variety quality. They are competing to fit inside distribution channels controlled by the companies that already sit at the top of broader indices. The shelf you see at the supply store reflects the weight those companies carry downstream.

Consider the agricultural input market from the perspective of someone standing at a farm gate choosing what to plant and how. The global seed market is dominated by four companies. Four companies control the majority of commercial seed sales for the major row crops — corn, soy, wheat, cotton. A farmer selecting a hybrid is selecting from a menu that these four companies designed, priced, and made available through distribution networks they also control. The choice of which corn variety to plant is real, and a good agronomist can find legitimate differences between fields. But the genetic foundation of more than half the corn planted in the United States traces back to breeding stock controlled by the same four entities. The range of available traits, the pricing structure around each trait, and the contractual restrictions on what a farmer can do with harvested seed — those are decisions made within a market structure that no single farmer can exit.

That pattern extends across the agricultural input chain. The global equipment market for large farm machinery is led by two companies that together control a dominant share of the combine and tractor markets for operations above a certain scale. Their parts, financing networks, and dealer territories define the economics of equipment maintenance for millions of acres. When those two companies converge on a feature set — say, closed proprietary diagnostics or subscription-based software unlocks — the dealers who service them cannot circumvent that design. The farmers who buy into the ecosystem cannot step outside it without trading decades of dealer relationships, localized knowledge, and parts availability. The tool — a tractor with advanced autonomy features — arrived with a promise of efficiency. The system surrounding it arrived with a promise of margin expansion for the manufacturers and their authorized channels.

The connection between agricultural consolidation and digital platform consolidation is structural, not aesthetic. Both follow the same pricing logic: get users onto the platform at low or zero cost, collect the data that makes switching expensive, and monetize the other side of the market.

That is not a prediction that every company playing this game succeeds. Companies in concentrated markets can still fail, compete aggressively, get displaced by better capital allocation, or get broken up by regulators who have the political will at the right moment. But the market structure itself does not correct toward more concentration by itself. Without competitive pressure from new entrants or regulatory intervention, concentrated markets tend to accumulate weight toward their largest players until the question of who controls a tool stops being meaningful.

On the ground, this means the tools you select — whether a seed variety or a cloud platform — arrive wrapped in a layer of upstream decisions you did not make and cannot undo. The farmer who buys a truckload of hybrid seed is not entering a free market the way the farmer who bought seed from the previous harvest was. The data center operator choosing a cloud provider is not choosing between independent vendors. The tool on the shelf is neutral in the way the brief mentions. What determines its shape, its constraints, and who benefits from its use is the concentration behind it.

The tool survives because the platform sustains it. The platform survives because someone on the far side is paying. That someone might be a subscriber whose money goes toward keeping the service running. It might be an advertiser whose campaigns fund the free tier. It might be a data broker trading in the behaviors your usage patterns reveal. But someone always pays, and the tool's design reflects that revenue dependency at every turn.

If you want to see the mechanism clearly, start with the people who are not paying for the tool. Take a free digital tool you use regularly. Look at who keeps it running. If it is not you, the incentive driving its development points toward what that other party values. The tool gets features that serve that party. It loses features that would help you but cost the platform something — data portability comes to mind, or the ability to export without friction. It gains features that make switching harder, because the value of the platform to its paying side depends on your continued presence.

This is not a moral judgment on any individual platform or founder. The same logic applies whether the paying side is an advertiser, an enterprise customer, or a financial institution. Two-sided markets have a built-in asymmetry: the side that does not pay is always treated as a resource. Platform economics works by balancing prices between the two sides — make one side so cheap that it keeps growing, charge the other side what the market will bear. The free tool you love exists because someone else funds it, and its feature roadmap is shaped by whoever writes the checks.

The platform has no loyalty to you. It has a margin requirement, a growth trajectory, and a board that will trade the relationship when the numbers suggest a better path. The tool is neutral about all of this. It does what its business model demands.

You should expect the primary function of a tool to diverge from what the company tells you it is. The divergence is not a bug or a deviation from the original plan. It is the equilibrium of the incentive structure. The more concentration exists upstream, the more the divergence hardens into a constraint, because there are fewer alternative platforms willing to offer different trade-offs. When every free tool follows the same two-sided playbook, refusing one platform means switching to another platform with the same playbook and a different face.

That does not mean every tool you pick up is compromised. It means the default posture should be to ask what the tool's survival depends on, not whether the tool is "good" or "bad" in the abstract. The design of the tool is a legible trace of the incentives that produced it. If the incentives are misaligned with your goals, no amount of individual virtue around how you use it corrects the alignment. Your usage patterns feed the platform. Your data deepens the moat around the concentration. Your preferences become inputs for the model that decides what features ship next.

The people who built the tool may have started with honest intentions. The ones who are paid to steer it next quarter are answering to different constraints. Those constraints accumulate. They shape the default settings. They shape the pricing tiers. They shape what features get promoted and what features get buried. They shape the partnerships that get announced and the ones that get quietly sunset. None of it requires malice. It only requires scale.

Once you see the mechanism, you stop asking whether the tool is neutral and start tracing who it serves. The answers take practice and a willingness to read the fine print on terms of service, privacy policies, and product roadmaps. Most tools will survive the scrutiny with a clean bill of health for the specific use case you need. Others will not. The point is not to distrust every product. The point is to see the architecture and make selections from that awareness instead of the illusion of a neutral marketplace.

---

Tools often arrive with a legitimate reason to exist. People need them. They solve an actual problem — productivity, communication, access — and the original use case is real and worth defending.

That is true. The drift that follows does not retroactively make the first use case fake.

What happens next is shaped by incentives and is exactly what the system you are in would be expected to produce. A tool built for a specific purpose encounters the same pricing logic that concentrates markets. The side of the market that gets priced is the user side, because that is the cheaper margin to extract. The tool either adds paid tiers for the people who need its original function, or it shifts toward a different function — one that generates revenue from a side of the market that does not pay at all.

That side is usually your attention.

The migration goes like this, without anyone needing to execute a conspiracy. The team that built the tool starts with a legible problem: someone needs to schedule a meeting, or coordinate a team, or automate a repetitive task. The free tier works well for that purpose. Then the revenue constraint hits. The two-sided model kicks in. The path of least resistance is to add features that increase engagement from the free side or sell the output to an advertiser who wants that audience. The tool does not break. It evolves toward the incentives that sustain it.

That is a predictable output of the system, not an accidental betrayal by the original builders. Both the use case and the drift are real. The original purpose is not a lie. The subsequent shift is not a betrayal of that purpose — it is the purpose colliding with the economics of the market the tool landed in.

This pattern appears everywhere you look. Smartphones started as communication devices and became engagement machines within a generation. Smart home gadgets arrive promising convenience and end up requiring subscriptions to turn off lights. The same sequence shows up in digital collaboration tools: free tier, data collection, upsell, feature bloat for the paying side, quiet degradation for the free tier, churn cycle. Each step is perfectly rational from the perspective of whoever controls the product roadmap. Each step moves the tool further from its original purpose.

One place this matters most, and where the neutrality framing does the most damage, is in assistive technology and access tools. Many disabled people already rely on adaptive pipelines built from AI and its precursors — speech-to-text, text-to-speech, real-time captioning, communication aids, visual description tools, cognitive supports, workflow automation for days when spoons are low. Public debate about AI frequently skips this layer entirely, flattening "AI" into a single monolith and letting the people who would benefit most from the technology get lost in the noise.

The tools that disabled people depend on often begin with a legible, necessary purpose. A communication app starts because someone needs to talk when they cannot type. A captioning service develops because someone needs to read a conversation they cannot hear. These are not convenience features. They are the difference between participation and exclusion.

The tools that disabled people depend on also face the same drift mechanics. When accessibility tools become commercially valuable, the incentive structure can shift in unexpected directions. A text-to-speech service that began as a free accommodation becomes an upsell tier. A captioning tool that works for a disabled user's needs gets replaced by a "creative video feature" designed for content creators. The disability use case does not disappear — it just becomes secondary to the metrics the platform optimized for.

This is not a reason to refuse the technology. The access use case is real, and the tool still works for people who need it. The point of tracing the mechanism is to understand that the tool you depend on exists in a system that can change its terms at any point. The platform might raise prices, sunset a free tier, or pivot the entire product. Disabled users who rely on these tools face a second-layer risk: the tools they need for basic participation are subject to market dynamics they did not design and cannot influence.

Understanding this does not mean waiting for something perfect, or building from scratch, or refusing what you need because the ecosystem is imperfect. It means recognizing that access, like land and infrastructure, is easiest to keep when you control its production. The tools you build or hold yourself cannot be pruned by a roadmap team optimizing for the wrong metric. They cannot vanish because a platform shifted its business model. They cannot be priced out because their user base became unprofitable.

The point is not to abandon tools that serve you. The point is to know which ones you need to own before the people who control them decide you are no longer their primary customer.

The original purpose of a tool is real. The drift toward novelty, engagement, and upsell is also real. Both can coexist. The question for anyone building their own exit ramp is which of those directions serves the thing they are trying to build, and which one will pull the tool away from that thing the moment the incentives shift.

Once you stop seeing tools as neutral objects and start tracking the incentives that surround them, the landscape changes in a practical way. You stop asking "is this tool good or bad?" and start asking "who benefits when this tool succeeds at its primary function?" You look at the revenue model before the feature list. You watch which side of the market pays, and you note what happens to the users who don't. You trace what a tool becomes when its original purpose runs into the economics of the market it landed in.

This is the same analytical move that shows up in Ch.2 — question your posture, not just your tools — applied at the market level rather than the individual tool level. Ch.2 looked at why the purity question is a trap. Ch.3 looks at why the neutrality question is a trap. Both lead to the same place: the decisions that matter are not about the objects you use, but about the structures that decide which objects exist, who can afford them, and what happens when the incentives change.

You can hold that truth and still choose tools wisely. Understanding incentives does not require paranoia. It requires looking past what the tool claims to be toward what it is designed to do and who keeps it alive. The next layer of this question is simpler than it sounds: what happens when the whole society treats tool blame as a substitute for the harder work of examining design? Society does that constantly. The habit is useful to anyone who profits from keeping you focused on the wrong target.
